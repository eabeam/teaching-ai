# Curated Papers: Teacher Quality and Student Outcomes

> These are verified real citations as of 2026. Use these as a reference set for the AI-assisted literature review exercise in Module C1.

## 1. Chetty, Friedman, and Rockoff (2014)

**Full citation**: Chetty, Raj, John N. Friedman, and Jonah E. Rockoff. 2014. "Measuring the Impacts of Teachers I: Evaluating Bias in Teacher Value-Added Estimates." *American Economic Review* 104(9): 2593-2632.

**Main finding**: Teacher value-added (VA) measures are not substantially biased by student sorting. When a high-VA teacher enters a school, student test scores rise immediately, and when they leave, scores drop, confirming that VA captures causal teacher effects rather than selection.

**Key method/identification**: Quasi-experimental design exploiting teacher turnover across schools. Compares changes in student outcomes when high- and low-VA teachers move in or out of a school, using the timing of moves as a natural experiment.

## 2. Chetty, Friedman, and Rockoff (2014)

**Full citation**: Chetty, Raj, John N. Friedman, and Jonah E. Rockoff. 2014. "Measuring the Impacts of Teachers II: Teacher Value-Added and Student Outcomes in Adulthood." *American Economic Review* 104(9): 2633-2679.

**Main finding**: Students assigned to higher-VA teachers are more likely to attend college, earn higher salaries, live in higher-income neighborhoods, save more for retirement, and are less likely to have children as teenagers. Replacing a teacher in the bottom 5% with an average teacher increases the present value of students' lifetime earnings by approximately $250,000 per classroom.

**Key method/identification**: Links school district administrative data with federal tax records to track long-run outcomes. Uses the same quasi-experimental variation from teacher turnover validated in the companion paper (Part I).

## 3. Rivkin, Hanushek, and Kain (2005)

**Full citation**: Rivkin, Steven G., Eric A. Hanushek, and John F. Kain. 2005. "Teachers, Schools, and Academic Achievement." *Econometrica* 73(2): 417-458.

**Main finding**: Teacher quality is the most important school-based determinant of student achievement. The variation in teacher quality within a school is substantially larger than variation across schools. A one-standard-deviation increase in teacher quality raises student test scores by roughly 0.1 standard deviations.

**Key method/identification**: Uses panel data on students in Texas to estimate teacher fixed effects while controlling for student, school, and family characteristics. Exploits within-school, across-cohort variation to separate teacher effects from school effects.

## 4. Kane, Rockoff, and Staiger (2008)

**Full citation**: Kane, Thomas J., Jonah E. Rockoff, and Douglas O. Staiger. 2008. "What Does Certification Tell Us about Teacher Effectiveness? Evidence from New York City." *Economics of Education Review* 27(6): 615-631.

**Main finding**: Traditional teacher certification requirements (such as having a master's degree or passing certification exams) are not strong predictors of teacher effectiveness as measured by student test score gains. Differences in teacher effectiveness are large but poorly predicted by observable credentials.

**Key method/identification**: Uses administrative data from New York City public schools matching teachers to students. Estimates teacher value-added from student achievement gains and examines how well certification status and other observable teacher characteristics predict these estimated effects.

## 5. Jackson (2018)

**Full citation**: Jackson, C. Kirabo. 2018. "What Do Test Scores Miss? The Importance of Teacher Effects on Non-Test Score Outcomes." *Journal of Political Economy* 126(5): 2072-2107.

**Main finding**: Teachers who improve student test scores are not the same teachers who improve non-test-score outcomes (such as absences, suspensions, course grades, and on-time grade progression). Teacher effects on non-cognitive outcomes are at least as important as teacher effects on test scores for predicting long-run outcomes like high school completion, college-going, and earnings.

**Key method/identification**: Uses administrative data from North Carolina linking students to teachers across multiple years. Estimates separate teacher value-added measures for test scores and non-test-score outcomes, then examines which measures better predict students' long-run outcomes.
