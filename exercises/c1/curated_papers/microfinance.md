# Curated Papers: Microfinance Impacts

## 1. Banerjee, Duflo, Glennerster, and Kinnan (2015)

**Full citation**: Banerjee, Abhijit, Esther Duflo, Rachel Glennerster, and Cynthia Kinnan. 2015. "The Miracle of Microfinance? Evidence from a Randomized Evaluation." *American Economic Journal: Applied Economics* 7(1): 22-53.

**Main finding**: Access to microcredit in urban Hyderabad, India led to a modest increase in business creation and business investment, but had no significant effect on average consumption, health, education, or women's empowerment after 15-18 months. The effects were concentrated among households with existing businesses. There was no evidence of the transformative impacts often attributed to microfinance.

**Key method/identification**: Randomized controlled trial. Randomly selected neighborhoods in Hyderabad were assigned to receive a new microfinance branch (Spandana), with remaining neighborhoods serving as controls. Compares outcomes across treatment and control areas using intent-to-treat estimates.

## 2. Crepon, Devoto, Duflo, and Pariente (2015)

**Full citation**: Crepon, Bruno, Florencia Devoto, Esther Duflo, and William Pariente. 2015. "Estimating the Impact of Microcredit on Those Who Take It Up: Evidence from a Randomized Experiment in Morocco." *American Economic Journal: Applied Economics* 7(1): 123-150.

**Main finding**: Microcredit access in rural Morocco expanded self-employment activity and investment in assets (particularly livestock), but did not increase overall income or consumption. Households used loans to scale up existing activities rather than start new businesses. There were no significant effects on women's empowerment or children's schooling.

**Key method/identification**: Randomized controlled trial at the village level in rural Morocco. Villages were randomly assigned to receive access to group lending through Al Amana. Uses village-level randomization with individual-level analysis, estimating both intent-to-treat and local average treatment effects (using random assignment as an instrument for take-up).

## 3. Meager (2019)

**Full citation**: Meager, Rachael. 2019. "Understanding the Average Impact of Microcredit Expansions: A Bayesian Hierarchical Analysis of Seven Randomized Experiments." *American Economic Journal: Applied Economics* 11(1): 57-91.

**Main finding**: Pooling evidence from seven randomized evaluations of microcredit, the average impact on household business profit is modestly positive but unlikely to be transformative, and the average impact on consumption is not significantly different from zero. There is substantial heterogeneity across study sites, meaning the impact of microcredit depends heavily on context.

**Key method/identification**: Bayesian hierarchical modeling applied to individual-level data from seven RCTs (including the Banerjee et al. and Crepon et al. studies above). Estimates both the average treatment effect across sites and the variance of treatment effects between sites, allowing formal inference about generalizability.

## 4. Kaboski and Townsend (2012)

**Full citation**: Kaboski, Joseph P., and Robert M. Townsend. 2012. "The Impact of Credit on Village Economies." *American Economic Journal: Applied Economics* 4(2): 98-133.

**Main finding**: A large-scale government credit program in Thailand (the Million Baht Village Fund) significantly increased short-term consumption, particularly for durable goods, and increased wages in treated villages. However, it did not increase business investment or asset accumulation, suggesting the credit was used for consumption smoothing rather than productive investment.

**Key method/identification**: Exploits the policy rule that each Thai village received 1 million baht regardless of village size. Uses village population size as an instrument for per-capita credit, since smaller villages received more credit per capita. Combines this with difference-in-differences across survey waves.

## 5. Angelucci, Karlan, and Zinman (2015)

**Full citation**: Angelucci, Manuela, Dean Karlan, and Jonathan Zinman. 2015. "Microcredit Impacts: Evidence from a Randomized Microcredit Program Placement Experiment." *American Economic Journal: Applied Economics* 7(1): 151-182.

**Main finding**: Expanding access to group lending in northeastern Mexico through Compartamos Banco led to increases in borrowing but no significant average effects on business outcomes, household income, labor supply, consumption, or subjective well-being. The results hold across a wide range of outcomes and subgroups.

**Key method/identification**: Cluster-randomized controlled trial. Geographic clusters were randomly assigned to receive new Compartamos Banco lending groups or to serve as controls. Uses intent-to-treat analysis comparing outcomes across treatment and control clusters.
