# Writing Example: Policy Implications Paragraph

This example shows three versions of a policy implications paragraph from a hypothetical study on conditional cash transfers and school enrollment in Kenya.

---

## Student Original

Our results suggest that the CCT program increased primary school enrollment by 6.2 percentage points in the treatment villages. But I'd be cautious about generalizing. The program ran in western Kenya during a period of relatively stable food prices, and the transfer amount (1,500 KES/month) was about 15% of median household income. If you tried the same thing during a food price crisis, or with a smaller transfer, or in an urban setting where the opportunity cost of schooling is higher, the effect might be totally different. I also want to flag that we only measure enrollment, not attendance or learning — and there's a growing literature showing those can diverge pretty sharply (see Bold et al. 2018).

---

## AI Full Rewrite

The findings of this study carry important policy implications for the design and implementation of conditional cash transfer programs in developing countries. The significant positive effect on primary school enrollment suggests that financial incentives can effectively promote educational attainment among disadvantaged populations. Policymakers should consider scaling up similar programs to other regions and contexts, while being mindful of the need to adapt transfer amounts and conditionality structures to local circumstances. It is also worth noting that enrollment gains do not necessarily translate into improved learning outcomes, and future program design should incorporate measures to ensure that increased enrollment is accompanied by improvements in educational quality. These results contribute to the growing evidence base supporting the use of CCTs as a tool for human capital development in low- and middle-income countries.

---

## AI-Assisted Targeted Edit

The CCT program increased primary school enrollment by 6.2 percentage points in treatment villages, but several factors limit the generalizability of this finding. The program operated in rural western Kenya during a period of stable food prices, and the transfer (1,500 KES/month, roughly 15% of median household income) was relatively generous. The same program in an urban setting, during a food crisis, or with a smaller transfer size could produce substantially different results. Additionally, we measure enrollment only — not attendance or learning. The distinction matters: Bold et al. (2018) document large gaps between enrollment and actual learning outcomes in similar contexts. Policymakers considering similar programs should attend to these boundary conditions rather than treating the estimated effect as directly portable.

**Key lesson**: The AI rewrite does exactly what the module warns about — it adds unwarranted generalization. The student was careful to say "this worked here, under these specific conditions, and here's why it might not transfer." The AI smoothed that into "policymakers should consider scaling up," which is not supported by a single-site study. The targeted edit keeps the student's appropriate caution while making the caveats more precise and actionable.
