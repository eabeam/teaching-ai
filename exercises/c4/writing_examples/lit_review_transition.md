# Writing Example: Literature Review Transition

This example shows three versions of a paragraph transitioning between two papers in a literature review about neighborhood effects on economic mobility.

---

## Student Original

Chetty et al. (2014) showed that where you grow up really matters — children who move to better neighborhoods before age 13 earn significantly more as adults. But their evidence is about moving *within* the existing distribution of neighborhoods. It doesn't tell us how that distribution got so unequal in the first place. That's where Derenoncourt (2022) comes in. She shows that the Great Migration — when millions of Black Americans moved from the South to Northern cities between 1940 and 1970 — caused White residents to leave for the suburbs, and this "white flight" permanently reduced upward mobility in the receiving cities. So the neighborhoods that Chetty et al. find matter so much for kids today were partly shaped by a migration shock that happened decades earlier.

---

## AI Full Rewrite

Research on neighborhood effects has demonstrated the significant role that geographic context plays in shaping economic outcomes. Chetty, Hendren, and Kline (2014) provide compelling evidence that childhood neighborhood exposure has substantial causal effects on adult earnings, with the magnitude of these effects varying based on the age at which families relocate. Building on this foundational work, Derenoncourt (2022) examines the historical origins of neighborhood inequality by investigating the long-run consequences of the Great Migration. Her findings suggest that the internal migration of African Americans to Northern cities during the mid-twentieth century contributed to patterns of residential segregation and reduced intergenerational mobility in affected areas. Together, these studies highlight the importance of considering both contemporaneous neighborhood effects and their historical determinants in understanding spatial inequality in economic mobility.

---

## AI-Assisted Targeted Edit

Chetty et al. (2014) showed that where you grow up matters: children who move to higher-opportunity neighborhoods before age 13 earn significantly more as adults. But their evidence is about mobility *within* the existing distribution of neighborhoods — it doesn't explain how that distribution became so unequal. Derenoncourt (2022) addresses this gap. She finds that the Great Migration, which brought millions of Black Americans from the South to Northern cities between 1940 and 1970, triggered white flight to the suburbs and permanently reduced upward mobility in the receiving cities. The connection is direct: the neighborhood quality that Chetty et al. show matters so much for children today was partly determined by a migration shock that happened decades earlier.

---

## What to Notice

| Feature | Student Original | AI Rewrite | Targeted Edit |
|---------|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| Clear logical connection between papers | Yes | Vague ("building on") | Yes |
| Specific mechanisms named | Yes (white flight) | Hedged ("patterns of segregation") | Yes |
| Dates and magnitudes | Yes | Yes but less specific | Yes |
| Explains *why* paper B follows paper A | Yes (gap in A that B fills) | Generic ("examines historical origins") | Yes |
| Student's own synthesis | Yes ("So the neighborhoods...") | Generic summary sentence | Yes |
| First-person or active engagement | Yes ("really matters") | No | Yes ("matters") |
| Transition feels earned vs. formulaic | Earned | Formulaic ("Building on...") | Earned |

**Key lesson**: The student's original transition does something the AI rewrite doesn't — it explains the intellectual gap between the two papers and why the second paper fills it. The AI version connects them with generic bridging language ("Building on this foundational work") that could link any two papers. The targeted edit preserves the student's logical structure while tightening the prose.
