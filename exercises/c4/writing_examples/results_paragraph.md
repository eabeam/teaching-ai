# Writing Example: Results Paragraph

This example shows three versions of a results paragraph from a hypothetical study evaluating a job training program's effect on employment.

---

## Student Original

The main result is that the training program increased employment by 8.3 percentage points (SE = 2.1, p < 0.01) relative to the control group mean of 54%. This is a pretty big effect — it means about 1 in 12 control group members who would not have been employed actually got a job because of the program. The effect was larger for women (11.2 pp) than for men (4.9 pp), though the difference isn't statistically significant (p = 0.14). I was surprised that there was basically no effect on earnings conditional on employment (coefficient of 340 BDT per month, not significant), which suggests the program helped people find jobs but not better-paying ones.

---

## AI Full Rewrite

The estimation results indicate that participation in the job training program had a statistically significant positive effect on employment outcomes. Specifically, the treatment group exhibited an 8.3 percentage point increase in employment probability compared to the control group (p < 0.01). This finding is economically meaningful when considered in the context of the baseline employment rate. Heterogeneity analysis reveals that the program's impact was somewhat larger for female participants than for male participants, although this differential effect did not reach conventional levels of statistical significance. Interestingly, the program did not appear to have a significant impact on earnings among those who were employed, suggesting that while the intervention was effective at facilitating employment transitions, it may not have improved the quality of employment obtained.

---

## AI-Assisted Targeted Edit

The training program increased employment by 8.3 percentage points (SE = 2.1, p < 0.01) relative to a control group mean of 54%. In practical terms, roughly 1 in 12 untreated individuals who would otherwise be unemployed found a job because of the program. The effect was larger for women (11.2 pp) than for men (4.9 pp), though the difference is not statistically significant (p = 0.14). Notably, there was no detectable effect on earnings conditional on employment (340 BDT/month, not significant), suggesting the program helped people enter employment but did not improve job quality.

---

## What to Notice

| Feature | Student Original | AI Rewrite | Targeted Edit |
|---------|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| Specific magnitudes (8.3 pp, 54% baseline) | Yes | Partially (drops some) | Yes |
| Interprets the coefficient in real terms | Yes ("1 in 12") | Vaguely ("economically meaningful") | Yes |
| Reports subgroup results with p-value | Yes | Drops the p-value | Yes |
| Takes a position on what's surprising | Yes ("I was surprised") | No (says "interestingly") | Yes (says "notably") |
| Hedging language | Minimal | Heavy | Minimal |
| Voice and personality | Strong | Generic | Preserved |
| Precision about the economics | High | Lower | High |

**Key lesson**: The targeted edit (version 3) tightens the student's prose without losing the specificity, the magnitudes, or the interpretive voice. The full rewrite (version 2) smooths everything out but drops details, adds hedging, and replaces the student's actual interpretation with generic language.
