# Subfield Prompt Bank: Hallucination Exercise

**Module A1: What LLMs Actually Do**

## For Instructors

The hallucination exercise works best when students test citations in a field they know (or are learning). Choose the prompt below that is closest to your course content, or write your own following the same pattern.

Each prompt asks for academic citations on a well-studied empirical question. This makes verification straightforward: students can check Google Scholar, NBER, or journal websites to see whether the citations are real.

---

## Option 1: Labor Economics

> "Give me three academic citations on the effect of minimum wage on teen employment."

**Why this works**: The minimum wage literature is large, well-known, and features prominent debates (Card & Krueger vs. Neumark & Wascher). Students can verify whether cited papers are real and whether described findings align with the actual evidence.

---

## Option 2: Development Economics

> "Give me three academic citations on the effect of cash transfers on educational outcomes in Sub-Saharan Africa."

**Why this works**: Cash transfer evaluations are among the most-replicated results in development economics. Many RCTs have been published in top journals, making verification feasible. This is the default prompt used in the module.

---

## Option 3: Health Economics

> "Give me three academic citations on the effect of Medicaid expansion on emergency room utilization."

**Why this works**: The Oregon Health Insurance Experiment and ACA Medicaid expansion studies are widely cited. Students can check whether the AI generates real papers or plausible-sounding fabrications using real author names.

---

## Option 4: International Trade

> "Give me three academic citations on the effect of Chinese import competition on US manufacturing employment."

**Why this works**: The "China shock" literature (Autor, Dorn, and Hanson) is well-defined and frequently cited. It is easy to verify whether the AI produces the real papers or invents similar-sounding ones.

---

## Writing Your Own Prompt

If none of the above fits your course, write a prompt that:

1. Asks for **specific academic citations** (not just "tell me about")
2. Targets a **well-studied empirical question** (so students can verify)
3. Is **narrow enough** that the AI will produce specific papers (not a vague overview)
4. Covers a topic your students have **enough background to evaluate**

**Template**:

> "Give me three academic citations on the effect of [intervention/policy] on [outcome] in [context]."
