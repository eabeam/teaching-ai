Curated Papers: Immigration and Wages

These are verified real citations as of 2026. Use these as a reference set for the AI-assisted literature review exercise in Module C1.

1. Card (1990)

Full citation: Card, David. 1990. “The Impact of the Mariel Boatlift on the Miami Labor Market.” Industrial and Labor Relations Review 43(2): 245-257.

Main finding: The sudden influx of roughly 125,000 Cuban immigrants to Miami in 1980 (a 7% increase in the labor force) had virtually no effect on the wages or unemployment rates of less-skilled non-Cuban workers in Miami relative to comparison cities. This suggests that local labor markets can absorb large immigration shocks without substantial wage effects.

Key method/identification: Natural experiment using the Mariel Boatlift as an exogenous immigration shock. Compares labor market outcomes in Miami (the treated city) to outcomes in four comparison cities (Atlanta, Houston, Los Angeles, Tampa-St. Petersburg) using a difference-in-differences framework with CPS data.

2. Borjas (2003)

Full citation: Borjas, George J. 2003. “The Labor Demand Curve Is Downward Sloping: Reexamining the Impact of Immigration on the Labor Market.” Quarterly Journal of Economics 118(4): 1335-1374.

Main finding: Immigration significantly reduces the wages of competing native workers. A 10% increase in labor supply due to immigration reduces wages in the affected skill group by 3-4%. The negative wage effects are largest for workers at the bottom of the education distribution, particularly high school dropouts.

Key method/identification: National-level analysis using skill cells defined by education and experience. Argues that area-based studies (like Card 1990) underestimate wage effects because native workers move away from high-immigration areas, diffusing the impact nationally. Uses census data from 1960-2000 and exploits variation across education-experience cells over time.

3. Peri and Sparber (2009)

Full citation: Peri, Giovanni, and Chad Sparber. 2009. “Task Specialization, Immigration, and Wages.” American Economic Journal: Applied Economics 1(3): 135-169.

Main finding: Immigration induces native workers to reallocate toward occupations that are more communication-intensive and less manual-labor-intensive. This task specialization by natives in response to immigration mitigates wage competition and can explain why the negative wage effects of immigration on natives are smaller than standard models predict.

Key method/identification: Uses CPS and census data to measure changes in the task content of native-born workers’ occupations in response to immigration flows. Instruments for immigration using historical settlement patterns (shift-share instrument). Estimates how immigration changes the relative task allocation of natives across occupations.

4. Dustmann, Schonberg, and Stuhler (2016)

Full citation: Dustmann, Christian, Uta Schonberg, and Jan Stuhler. 2016. “The Impact of Immigration: Why Do Studies Reach Such Different Results?” Journal of Economic Literature 54(4): 1164-1214.

Main finding: Differences in results across immigration studies can be explained by differences in the identifying variation used, the comparison groups chosen, and the assumptions about how labor markets adjust. The paper provides a unifying framework showing that area-based, skill-cell-based, and structural approaches answer fundamentally different questions and rely on different assumptions about worker mobility and market adjustment.

Key method/identification: Review and synthesis paper that develops a formal framework nesting the major empirical approaches. Demonstrates how each approach’s identifying assumptions determine what parameter it estimates, explaining why Card (1990) and Borjas (2003) can both be “correct” while reaching apparently contradictory conclusions.

5. Clemens and Hunt (2019)

Full citation: Clemens, Michael A., and Jennifer Hunt. 2019. “The Labor Market Effects of Refugee Waves: Reconciling Conflicting Results.” ILR Review 72(4): 818-857.

Main finding: Revisits the Mariel Boatlift debate between Card (1990) and Borjas (2016, who found large negative wage effects for high school dropouts). Shows that the divergent findings are driven by compositional changes in the CPS sample of high school dropouts in Miami during this period, not by actual wage effects. After accounting for sample composition, the evidence is consistent with Card’s original finding of negligible wage effects.

Key method/identification: Replication and decomposition analysis. Demonstrates that changes in the racial composition of the small CPS sample of Miami high school dropouts drive Borjas’s results. Uses multiple datasets and sample definitions to test robustness of both sets of findings.
