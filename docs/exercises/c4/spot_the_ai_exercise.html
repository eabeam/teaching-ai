Spot the AI: Student vs. AI Writing

Instructions

Below are four paragraphs about the same topic: the effect of immigration on wages in the United States. Two were written by a student. Two were generated by AI.

Read all four paragraphs carefully. Then answer the questions at the bottom.

Paragraph A

The relationship between immigration and wages is a topic that has generated considerable debate among economists and policymakers alike. While some researchers have found evidence suggesting that immigration may exert downward pressure on wages for certain worker groups, others have documented minimal effects or even positive outcomes. It is important to note that the impact of immigration on wages depends on many factors, including the skill composition of immigrants, the structure of local labor markets, and the time horizon considered. Furthermore, the methodological approaches used to study this question vary significantly across studies, which may partly explain the divergent findings in the literature. Overall, the evidence suggests a nuanced picture in which the effects of immigration on wages are context-dependent and multifaceted.

Paragraph B

The Mariel Boatlift is still the most fought-over natural experiment in labor economics, and honestly, that tells you a lot about how hard this question is. Card (1990) found basically zero wage effects from a 7% labor supply shock in Miami, which should have settled things, but then Borjas came back in 2017 and found large negative effects for high school dropouts using the same event. The difference came down to how you define the comparison group and which CPS subsample you use — Clemens and Hunt (2019) showed that Borjas’s result was driven by compositional changes in a tiny sample. I think the bigger lesson is that when two serious economists can look at the same data and reach opposite conclusions, the answer probably depends on details that aggregate data can’t resolve.

Paragraph C

The impact of immigration on native wages represents a central question in labor economics, one that has important implications for both economic theory and public policy. Research in this area has evolved considerably over the past several decades, employing increasingly sophisticated identification strategies to address the inherent endogeneity concerns. Additionally, scholars have recognized the importance of distinguishing between short-run and long-run effects, as labor markets may adjust through various channels including capital accumulation, task specialization, and internal migration. Moreover, recent work has highlighted the heterogeneity of immigration impacts across different skill levels and geographic areas, suggesting that a one-size-fits-all characterization of the wage effects of immigration would be misleading.

Paragraph D

There is something weird about the immigration-wages literature that I didn’t appreciate until this semester. Borjas (2003) argues that area-based studies miss the effect because native workers move away from high-immigration cities, spreading the wage impact nationally. That’s a clever point. But if you take it seriously, it means Card-style natural experiments are fundamentally the wrong tool, and you’re stuck estimating off of national skill-cell variation, which has its own problems. Dustmann, Schonberg, and Stuhler (2016) basically argue that the two approaches answer different questions. I find that more convincing than either side claiming the other one is just wrong.

Questions

1. Identification (5 min)

Which two paragraphs were written by AI? Which two were written by a student?

  • AI-written: ___ and ___
  • Student-written: ___ and ___

2. Reasoning (5 min)

What features helped you identify the AI paragraphs? List at least three specific tells.

3. Compare the student paragraphs to the AI paragraphs

  • Which paragraphs cite specific papers and findings?
  • Which paragraphs express a personal interpretation or opinion?
  • Which paragraphs could have been written about almost any contested topic in economics?
Answer Key (click to reveal)

AI-written: A and C

Student-written: B and D

Key tells for the AI paragraphs:

  • Excessive hedging: “It is important to note that…”, “suggesting that a one-size-fits-all characterization would be misleading”
  • Generic transitions: “Furthermore,” “Additionally,” “Moreover,” stacked without adding content
  • False balance: Every claim is immediately hedged (“some researchers… others…”, “while… however…”)
  • Lack of specificity: No specific papers, magnitudes, or findings cited in A; C references “increasingly sophisticated identification strategies” without naming any
  • Could be about anything: Paragraph A could be about immigration and wages, trade and wages, minimum wages and employment, or almost any contested empirical question — just swap the nouns
  • Uniform tone: No personality, no first-person voice, no opinion, no moments of genuine engagement with the material
  • Smooth but empty: Both AI paragraphs read well but say almost nothing specific

Key features of the student paragraphs:

  • Specific citations with details: Card (1990), Borjas (2017), Clemens and Hunt (2019), Dustmann et al. (2016), Borjas (2003)
  • Actual engagement: “that tells you a lot about how hard this question is”, “I think the bigger lesson is…”, “I find that more convincing”
  • Willingness to take a position: The student has opinions about which arguments are stronger
  • Rough edges: Slightly informal phrasing, first-person voice, sentences that feel like someone thinking on paper
  • Specificity about methods: “7% labor supply shock”, “CPS subsample”, “national skill-cell variation”

4. Discussion

What features of AI writing should you watch for and avoid in your own AI-assisted work? How can you ensure your AI-assisted writing retains the specificity and voice that make writing useful?
