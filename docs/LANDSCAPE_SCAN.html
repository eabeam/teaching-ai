Landscape Scan: University Courses Teaching AI Skills to Economics/Social Science Students

Purpose: Assess originality and feasibility of a proposed 1-credit course on “AI in Economics” at the undergraduate level, focused on practical AI skills (prompting, GitHub, markdown, terminal, verification) for econ majors.

Search date: 2026-03-28

Summary of Findings

The landscape divides into four categories, ordered from most to least similar to the proposed course:

  1. Practical AI-for-economists tools courses (closest comparators – rare)
  2. General AI literacy courses/micro-credentials (growing rapidly – different focus)
  3. Economics-of-AI courses (common – fundamentally different)
  4. Professional/researcher resources (not courses per se, but occupy adjacent space)

Bottom line: No course identified combines all three of the proposed course’s distinctive features: (a) practical AI tool skills, (b) economics-specific applications, and (c) technical foundations like terminal, Git, and markdown. The closest comparators exist but differ in important ways. This course would occupy a genuine gap.

Tier 1: Closest Comparators

CEU Vienna — “Doing Data Analysis with AI”

  • Institution: Central European University, Vienna
  • Format: ~12 weeks, appears to be embedded in a program (credits unclear)
  • Audience: 2nd-3rd year BA and MA students in economics, quantitative social science, political science, sociology, history
  • Content: Using AI tools to improve productivity in data analysis tasks. Students must already know core data analysis methods. Emphasis on “AI is your assistant” – students must use AI but not submit AI-generated work.
  • Source: Gabor’s Data Analysis - AI Course
  • How similar: HIGH. This is the closest match in spirit – practical AI skills for social science students doing empirical work. However, it assumes existing coding/data skills and focuses on data analysis specifically, not on building technical foundations (terminal, Git) or career/research applications.
  • Key difference from proposed course: Assumes students already code. No terminal/Git/markdown instruction. Narrower scope (data analysis vs. full workflow).

Thiemo Fetzer, Warwick — “Machine Learning and AI in Economics”

  • Institution: University of Warwick, Economics Department
  • Format: Lecture-based course with research paper discussion
  • Audience: Economics students
  • Content: Basics of machine learning, information economics, language modeling, neural networks, transformer-based tools, and LLMs. Draws examples from research. Running commentary on tech/regulatory developments.
  • Source: Thiemo Fetzer - ML and AI in Economics
  • How similar: MODERATE. Covers AI from an economics perspective with applied examples, but is more theoretical/research-paper-oriented than skills-based. Not focused on students using AI tools hands-on.
  • Key difference: Theory/lecture-focused, not hands-on skills. No terminal, Git, or career applications.

Tier 2: General AI Literacy Courses (Different Focus but Relevant Format)

These are proliferating rapidly across universities. They share the “teach students to use AI” goal but are discipline-agnostic, and typically do not include the technical skill foundations (terminal, Git) or economics-specific applications.

University of Mary Washington — IDIS 300: Introduction to AI (1-credit)

  • Format: 1-credit, offered over summer at no cost. Optional for incoming/current students.
  • Content: AI ethics, tools, copyright concerns, career impacts. Also offered: DGST 301N “AI and Society” (deeper dive, students build chatbots, guest lectures).
  • Source: UMW Center for AI and the Liberal Arts
  • How similar: MODERATE on format (1-credit, short), LOW on content. Liberal arts framing, no economics-specific content, no technical skills.
  • Key takeaway: Precedent for 1-credit AI course accepted by a university. The “Center for AI and the Liberal Arts” model is interesting institutional framing.

UConn — “AI 4 All”

  • Format: Elective special topics, 1000/2000 level, no prerequisites. 20 sections of 25 students, 75-min sessions. Fall 2025.
  • Content: Generative AI, real-world applications, ethical considerations. Foundational understanding of AI’s role in learning, creativity, problem-solving.
  • Source: UConn Daily Campus
  • How similar: Similar format ambition (accessible, no prerequisites, broad enrollment). Very different content – no disciplinary application, no technical skills.

Ohio State — “Unlocking Generative AI” (ENGR 2194)

  • Format: 3-credit elective, open to all majors, designed for beginners.
  • Content: Demystifying generative AI, ethical issues, prompt engineering, real-world case studies. Part of a broader “AI Fluency” initiative requiring all undergrads to get AI basics.
  • Source: OSU AI Fluency
  • How similar: LOW-MODERATE. Much broader, 3 credits, not discipline-specific. But the “prompt engineering + ethics + applications” structure has parallels.

University of Pittsburgh — “AI Literacy: Foundations for Critical Thinking and Informed Use”

  • Format: Offered Spring 2026, Department of Informatics. Discussion-based.
  • Content: AI’s diverse applications in modern society. Ethics, influence on work, education, creativity. Does NOT center on building or programming – examines AI through a “people- and society-centered lens.”
  • Source: Pitt Spring 2026 New Courses
  • How similar: LOW. Explicitly avoids technical skills. Discussion/analysis focus, not hands-on.

NJIT — AI Literacy Microcredential

  • Format: Non-credit, ten 2-hour self-paced online courses. Free for NJIT students.
  • Content: Three units: Foundations of AI, Effective/Responsible Use (including prompting, critical evaluation), Technical Aspects (data proficiency, ML awareness, neural networks).
  • Source: NJIT AI Literacy
  • How similar: LOW-MODERATE. Closest in technical ambition among the literacy offerings (includes prompting, ML basics). But non-credit, self-paced, no disciplinary framing, no terminal/Git.

SDSU — Academic Applications of AI Micro-Credential

  • Format: Non-credit, five self-paced modules, 2-4 hours total. Free for SDSU students.
  • Content: How AI works, ethics/responsible use, finding AI apps, prompt engineering activities.
  • Source: SDSU AAAI
  • How similar: LOW. Very lightweight. No discipline-specific content. Basic prompt engineering only.

University of New Orleans — “Empowering AI Literacy” Micro-Credential

  • Format: Free, self-paced. Non-credit.
  • Content: AI literacy for navigating an AI-driven world.
  • Source: UNO AI Literacy
  • How similar: LOW. Generic literacy, no disciplinary application.

Tier 3: Economics-of-AI Courses (Common but Fundamentally Different)

These study AI as an economic phenomenon – market structure, labor effects, growth models. They do not teach students to use AI tools.

Stanford — “The Economics of Transformative AI” (Summer 2025)

  • Format: Two-week intensive, primarily for grad students. Lectures + exercises.
  • Content: Economic theory for evaluating claims about transformative AI: automation in production/growth models, task-based frameworks, research and innovation. Instructors: Phil Trammell, Zach Mazlish, with guest lectures by Erik Brynjolfsson and Chad Jones.
  • Source: Stanford Digital Economy Lab
  • How similar: LOW. This is a graduate theory course about AI’s macroeconomic implications. Not about using AI tools. Also available via BlueDot Impact.

UVA / Coursera — “The Economics of AI” (Korinek)

  • Format: Online, Coursera. Self-paced.
  • Content: Cutting-edge research on AI’s implications for economic growth and labor markets.
  • Source: Coursera - Economics of AI
  • How similar: LOW. Studies AI as an economic object, does not teach AI tool use.

UNH — ECON 652: Economics of Artificial Intelligence

  • Format: Standard semester course (appears graduate-level).
  • Content: Three sections – AI as a general-purpose technology, improved prediction for decision-making under uncertainty, AI’s impact on business/society/labor.
  • Source: UNH Course Search
  • How similar: LOW. Traditional economics-of-X course.

Yale — “Economics of Artificial Intelligence and Innovation” (Chalioti)

  • Source: Yale Cowles Foundation
  • How similar: LOW. Research-focused.

University at Buffalo — AI and Quantitative Economics (Program)

  • Format: Full degree program, not a single course.
  • Content: Macro/micro, AI effects on policymaking, econometrics, AI electives.
  • Source: UB Admissions
  • How similar: LOW. A full program, not a skills course.

Tier 4: Professional/Researcher Resources (Not Courses)

These are not formal university courses but occupy adjacent space and are likely known to economists considering this topic.

Korinek — genaiforecon.org

  • What it is: Companion website to Korinek’s JEL article “Generative AI for Economic Research” (JEL 2023). Semi-annual updates on AI tools/techniques. Covers use cases: literature review, coding, data analysis, writing, math, AI agents.
  • Source: genaiforecon.org
  • Relevance: The most widely cited guide for economists using AI. Graduate/researcher-focused. Not a course with learning objectives, assessments, or progression, but covers many of the same application areas. Updated to include AI agents (August 2025).

Lastunen — “AI for Economists”

  • What it is: A curated set of example prompts and LLM responses across six domains: ideation/feedback, writing, background research, coding, data analysis, math derivations. Development economics examples.
  • Source: Jesse Lastunen - AI for Economists
  • Relevance: Useful reference/inspiration for course content. Example-first approach. Not structured as a course.

AEI — “AI Tools for Economists and Policy Analysts”

  • What it is: A resource/guide on integrating AI tools into economic research workflows.
  • Source: AEI
  • Relevance: Professional audience, not pedagogical.

awesome-ai-for-economists (GitHub)

  • What it is: A curated list of AI tools, libraries, and resources for economics research, teaching, and policy analysis.
  • Source: GitHub - hanlulong/awesome-ai-for-economists
  • Relevance: Useful resource list. Not a course.

Bristol Working Paper — “AI and the Future of Work for Economists: Rethinking Economics Education”

  • What it is: Academic paper (Oschinski, Spielmann, Subbu-Rathinam) using Lightcast data to analyze how AI impacts occupations entered by economics graduates. Implications for curriculum design.
  • Source: Bristol Economics Discussion Paper 25/788
  • Relevance: Provides empirical motivation for why a course like this matters. Useful to cite in a course proposal.

Distinctively novel elements (no clear precedent found):

  1. Technical foundations for economists: No identified course teaches terminal, Git, and markdown to economics undergrads as AI-complementary skills. This is a genuine gap – the MODULE_CATALOG notes “almost no econ programs teach this.”

  2. Peer teaching / student-led workshop component: No identified course includes a peer-teaching or student-led demonstration model.

  3. 1-credit format with economics-specific AI skills: The UMW 1-credit precedent exists for general AI literacy, but combining short format with economics-specific hands-on content is novel.

  4. Career preparation through AI: E1 (job market prep) has no clear course analogue. Some AI literacy courses mention careers generically, but none integrate this with economics career paths.

  5. Integrated stack (prompting + verification + terminal + Git + Stata/R AI assistance + career prep): No single course bundles all of these. The CEU course comes closest but omits the technical foundations and career elements.

Elements with clear precedents (course would not be first):

  1. Prompting frameworks: Widely taught (SDSU, NJIT, Ohio State, Coursera courses, Korinek resources).
  2. AI ethics/integrity: Covered in nearly every AI literacy offering.
  3. AI for coding assistance: CEU course, Korinek resources, various CS-adjacent courses.
  4. AI for data analysis: CEU course covers this directly.
  5. AI for writing/revision: Korinek, Lastunen, and multiple resources cover this.

Feasibility Signals

Positive signals: - Universities are rapidly adopting AI literacy requirements (Ohio State, UConn, UMW) – administrative appetite for these courses is high - The 1-credit / micro-credential format is clearly viable and growing - No close competitor means differentiation is easy - The Bristol working paper provides empirical ammunition for why economics students specifically need this - Economics departments have not been early movers on this – most activity is from engineering, informatics, or campus-wide initiatives

Potential challenges: - Fast-moving content requires maintenance commitment (the MODULE_CATALOG already accounts for this) - May need to justify to a curriculum committee why this belongs in economics rather than computer science or a general education requirement - The “1-credit” format constrains how much can be covered – will need to select carefully from the 39-module catalog

Recommended Next Steps

  1. Read the Bristol working paper (Oschinski et al.) for quantitative evidence on which AI skills economics graduates need – useful for a course proposal.
  2. Review the CEU “Doing Data Analysis with AI” materials in detail at gabors-data-analysis.com/ai-course/ – closest content comparator.
  3. Check Korinek’s genaiforecon.org for current best-practice examples that could inform module content.
  4. Look for the UMW course proposal documents – they navigated the 1-credit approval process and could provide a template.
  5. Search the AEA’s EDUCATE workshop archives for any presentations on teaching AI skills to economics students.
